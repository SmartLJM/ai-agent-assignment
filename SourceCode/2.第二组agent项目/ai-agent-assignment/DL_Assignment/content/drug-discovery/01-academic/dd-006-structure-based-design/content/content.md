# Structure-Based Drug Design

**Knowledge set ID:** `dd-006-structure-based-design`  
**Theme:** `drug-discovery`  
**Source:** Computational methods in drug discovery  

## Curated Content

Structure-based drug design uses the three-dimensional structure of a biological target to design or
select ligands for a binding site. Structures can come from X-ray crystallography, cryo-electron
microscopy, NMR spectroscopy, or high-confidence computational models. The method examines shape
complementarity, hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic contacts, electrostatics, water networks, and induced
fit.  Structure-based design can support virtual screening, fragment growing, scaffold replacement,
selectivity analysis, and lead optimization. It is especially useful when the binding site is known
and structural data are reliable. However, docking scores are approximations and should not be
treated as proof of binding or efficacy.  A complete answer should explain both the strength and
limitation of structure-based approaches: they provide mechanistic hypotheses about molecular
recognition, but experimental binding, functional assays, and safety studies remain necessary.
