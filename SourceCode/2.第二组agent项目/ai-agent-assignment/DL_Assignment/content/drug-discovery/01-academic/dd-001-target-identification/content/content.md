# Target Identification in Drug Discovery

**Knowledge set ID:** `dd-001-target-identification`  
**Theme:** `drug-discovery`  
**Source:** Principles of early drug discovery  

## Curated Content

Target identification is the selection of a disease-relevant biological molecule, pathway, or
cellular process that can be modified to produce a therapeutic effect. A strong target is not simply
associated with a disease; it should have evidence that changing the target can causally improve the
disease phenotype. Evidence can come from human genetics, genomics, proteomics, pathway biology,
patient data, perturbation experiments, and literature mining.  In computational drug discovery,
target identification is often supported by disease networks, omics analysis, knowledge graphs, and
machine-learning models that connect genes, proteins, pathways, phenotypes, and compounds. The
target should also be druggable, meaning that a feasible therapeutic modality can modulate it.
Druggability may involve an enzyme pocket, a receptor binding site, a protein-protein interaction
surface, an RNA target, or a biologic modality such as an antibody.  Poor target choice is a major
reason for downstream failure. Therefore, target identification should connect disease biology,
mechanistic plausibility, patient relevance, and therapeutic feasibility before expensive screening
or lead optimization begins.
