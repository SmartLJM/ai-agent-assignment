# Pharmacodynamics

**Knowledge set ID:** `dd-013-pharmacodynamics`  
**Theme:** `drug-discovery`  
**Source:** Clinical Pharmacology and Pharmacodynamics overview  

## Curated Content

Pharmacodynamics describes what a drug does to the body. It includes target engagement, receptor
binding, pathway modulation, mechanism of action, dose-response relationships, efficacy, potency,
biomarkers, and toxicity mechanisms. Pharmacodynamics links drug exposure to biological effect.  A
pharmacodynamic effect may be measured with functional assays, biomarkers, imaging, clinical
endpoints, or surrogate endpoints. The relationship between dose, exposure, target occupancy, and
response can be nonlinear. More dose is not always better because efficacy may plateau while
toxicity increases.  A correct answer should explain that pharmacodynamics complements
pharmacokinetics. Pharmacokinetics asks whether the compound reaches the body compartments at
sufficient exposure, whereas pharmacodynamics asks whether that exposure produces the desired
biological response safely.
