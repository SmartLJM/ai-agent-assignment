# Pharmacokinetics

**Knowledge set ID:** `dd-012-pharmacokinetics`  
**Theme:** `drug-discovery`  
**Source:** Clinical Pharmacology and Pharmacokinetics overview  

## Curated Content

Pharmacokinetics describes what the body does to a drug over time. It covers absorption,
distribution, metabolism, and excretion as dynamic processes after dosing. Common pharmacokinetic
parameters include half-life, clearance, volume of distribution, bioavailability, maximum
concentration, time to maximum concentration, and area under the concentration-time curve.
Pharmacokinetics helps determine dose, dosing interval, route of administration, and whether
exposure is sufficient for the desired therapeutic effect. It also helps evaluate drug-drug
interactions, food effects, organ impairment, and population differences.  In drug discovery,
pharmacokinetic optimization connects chemistry to real exposure. A compound with excellent in vitro
potency can fail if it has poor bioavailability, rapid clearance, or unsafe accumulation. A precise
answer should distinguish pharmacokinetics from pharmacodynamics, which asks what the drug does to
the body.
